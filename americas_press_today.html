🔭 What America's Press Sees That The World Misses

The opioid crisis never ended
International coverage of the US drug crisis peaked around 2017-2019. It never went away — it evolved. Hundreds of thousands of Americans have died since the world moved on. It remains the defining domestic tragedy of the era.
Local news collapse
Hundreds of American local newspapers have closed since 2020. "News deserts" — communities with no local coverage — now cover vast swathes of the country. The long-term democratic consequences are barely covered internationally.
America's housing crisis
The US has a housing shortage of roughly 4 million homes. Home ownership is increasingly unattainable for under-40s. This is as acute as anything in the UK or Spain — and receives a fraction of the political attention.
The state-level story
International coverage of the US focuses entirely on Washington. But abortion law, education policy, gun law, and voting rights are now set at state level — and the divergence between red and blue states is creating what are effectively two different countries.

🔭 Canada's Blind Spots

The sovereignty shock
Trump's "51st state" comments triggered an identity crisis unlike anything since the Quebec referendums. Canada is rediscovering its own national character in response to external threat. This is a profound sociological moment largely invisible internationally.
Western alienation
Alberta's frustration with Ottawa is structural, long-standing, and serious. The possibility of western Canadian separatism is not as remote as international observers assume. Canadian media covers this constantly. Foreign press treats it as curiosity.
The Indigenous reconciliation gap
Canada's Truth and Reconciliation commitments remain largely unfulfilled. The gap between official statements and lived reality on First Nations reserves is enormous — and covered with great seriousness in Canadian papers and almost none internationally.
Canada's housing crisis rivals London or Sydney
Price-to-income ratios in Vancouver and Toronto are among the worst in the developed world. This is as acute as anywhere — but Canada's generally positive international image means it receives far less global attention than equivalent crises elsewhere.

🔭 Mexico's Blind Spots

The cartels as parallel state
In significant parts of Mexico, cartels provide employment, dispute resolution, and infrastructure — functioning as a parallel government. This is covered seriously by Mexican journalists (at great personal risk) and almost not at all internationally beyond sensationalised violence coverage.
Mexico's missing persons crisis
Over 100,000 people are listed as disappeared in Mexico — a figure that dwarfs any similar crisis in Europe or North America. Families organising their own searches. This barely registers in international news.
The nearshoring revolution
Trump's tariffs on China have turned Mexico into the world's most attractive manufacturing location for US-bound goods. This is transforming the Mexican economy faster than any policy in decades. International coverage is thin.
Femicide
Mexico has among the highest femicide rates in the world. Women's movements, the "glitter protests," and the political response are major domestic stories. International coverage occasionally spikes then disappears entirely.

🔭 Brazil's Blind Spots

The Amazon beyond the headlines
International coverage treats the Amazon as a deforestation metric. Brazilians who live in and around it — riberinhos, quilombolas, Indigenous nations — experience it as a homeland, an economy, and a site of physical conflict with illegal miners and loggers that rarely makes national news. The forest's residents are almost entirely absent from both Brazilian and international coverage.
The favela as political actor
Brazil's largest cities are governed in parallel by elected officials and by criminal factions (CV, PCC, militias) that control territory, provide services, and extract taxes. This shadow governance — affecting tens of millions of Brazilians — is rarely examined as a structural feature of Brazilian democracy, rather than simply a crime story.
Judicial activism and institutional power
Brazil's Supreme Court (STF) has become an unusually powerful political actor — banning X, investigating Bolsonaro, ruling on electoral law. The debate about whether this represents democratic protection or judicial overreach is a live Brazilian political question that international coverage almost entirely ignores.
Agribusiness as civilisation
Brazil's agribusiness sector feeds the world — the largest exporter of soy, beef, chicken, sugar, and coffee. The political power of the ruralist bloc (bancada ruralista) in Congress shapes everything from environmental law to trade policy, but is understood internationally only through an environmental lens, not as the political and economic colossus it actually is.

🔭 Argentina's Blind Spots — What the world misses

The informal economy
Nearly half of Argentina's workforce operates in the informal sector — outside payroll taxes, labour protections, and official statistics. Every macroeconomic analysis of Argentina that ignores this is operating on half the picture. Milei's reforms hit formal workers hardest; informal workers were already living without state support.
Lithium triangle geopolitics
Argentina sits on the world's largest lithium reserves, critical for EV batteries. The US, China, and the EU are all competing for access. Milei's deregulation agenda is reshaping mining concessions in ways that will affect global clean energy supply chains for decades — a story almost entirely absent from international coverage of his presidency.
Agribusiness as political actor
Argentina's soya and beef export sector is one of the world's most powerful agricultural lobbies and has historically been the swing actor in Argentine political economy — supporting or undermining governments based on export tax policy. Its relationship with Milei (who cut export taxes) is a crucial part of understanding why his coalition holds together.

🔭 Colombia's Blind Spots

The peace deal's quiet unravelling
The 2016 FARC peace agreement was internationally celebrated. What's less covered: dissident FARC factions never signed, the ELN expanded into vacated FARC territory, and hundreds of former combatants and community leaders have been assassinated since signing. The peace is real in some regions; in others, armed groups have simply reshuffled.
Coca as rural economy
Colombia's coca cultivation is framed internationally as a drug war story. For the hundreds of thousands of campesino families who grow it — often in areas with no viable alternative cash crop and no state presence — it is simply a livelihood. Petro's crop substitution approach versus eradication is a genuine policy debate that gets lost behind the drug war framing.
Venezuela's collapse next door
Colombia has absorbed over 2.5 million Venezuelan migrants — the largest refugee crisis in the western hemisphere. This is transforming Colombian cities, labour markets, and communities in ways that dwarf anything in European migration debates, yet receives a fraction of the international attention.
Afro-Colombian and Indigenous communities
Colombia's Pacific coast — heavily Afro-Colombian — is among the most biodiverse and most violent places in the country. These communities face displacement, forced recruitment, and environmental destruction with almost no national political voice. They are nearly invisible in both Colombian and international media except during acute crises.

🔭 Chile's Blind Spots

Lithium and the new resource politics
Chile holds the world's largest lithium reserves — critical for EV batteries and the global energy transition. The debate over whether to nationalise, partner, or liberalise lithium extraction is one of the most consequential resource politics stories in Latin America, with China, the US, and the EU all competing for access. The Mapuche conflict
Chile's Araucanía region has been under a constitutional state of emergency for years, with Mapuche communities demanding land restitution and the state responding with security measures. This is not a fringe issue — it's a genuine unresolved territorial conflict involving 10% of the Chilean population that Santiago-centred coverage consistently underweights.
The constitutional question isn't settled
Chile rejected a new constitution in 2022, then rejected another in 2023. The underlying question — whether the Pinochet-era 1980 constitution remains legitimate — was not answered by either "No" vote. The political framework of Chilean democracy remains genuinely contested in a way that routine coverage of Boric's governance obscures.
Environmental cost of the export miracle
Chile's prosperity is built on copper, salmon farming, forestry, and agribusiness — all industries with major environmental footprints. The salmon farming industry in Patagonia has caused ecological damage that Chilean and international business coverage systematically downplays in favour of export figures and employment statistics.

Madre de Dios region has the world's highest mercury contamination in humans due to illegal artisanal gold mining. This is a major public health and environmental catastrophe that international coverage routinely ignores in favour of Brazil's deforestation story.
The 2022–23 protest dead
At least 49 people were killed in protests following Castillo's removal — disproportionately indigenous and from the southern highlands. The scale of this and the human rights implications were barely covered outside specialist outlets.
The world's copper supply runs through Andean conflict zones
Peru is the world's second-largest copper producer. Most of its key mines operate in areas of persistent community conflict and weak state presence — a structural risk to global supply chains that is under-reported relative to its strategic significance.
