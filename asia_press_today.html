🔭 Japan's Blind Spots

Demographic collapse in real time
Japan's population is shrinking and ageing faster than any large country in history. Towns are being abandoned, schools are closing, and the fiscal arithmetic of a society with more retirees than workers becomes more alarming each year. Japanese media covers this as a policy challenge; what gets less attention is the communities — rural, post-industrial — that are simply ceasing to exist.
Corporate Japan's shadow
Japan's major corporations are covered in business pages globally. What's less examined is the dense web of keiretsu relationships, cross-shareholdings, and informal corporate governance norms that mean Japanese capitalism operates quite differently from the shareholder-value model. Reform efforts repeatedly founder on these invisible structures.
Japan's foreign workers
Japan has quietly become a major immigration destination through "technical intern" and "specified skilled worker" visa categories that critics call indentured labour. Hundreds of thousands of Vietnamese, Filipino, and Indonesian workers sustain Japanese agriculture, construction, and care homes under conditions that rarely appear in mainstream coverage.
Nuclear restart and local democracy
Japan restarted nuclear reactors after Fukushima — a politically contentious process requiring approval from local governments whose communities live near plants. The gap between national energy policy (pro-restart) and the views of affected communities (deeply divided) is a democratic tension that gets lost in the global energy transition framing.

🔭 Taiwan's Blind Spots

TSMC is not Taiwan
International coverage of Taiwan increasingly reduces it to a semiconductor story — TSMC, chip supply chains, the "silicon shield." This flattens a vibrant democracy of 23 million people into a geopolitical chess piece. Taiwan's internal politics, social movements, generational debates about identity, and relationship with its own history are largely invisible outside this framing.
Indigenous Taiwan
Taiwan has 16 officially recognised Indigenous peoples — Amis, Atayal, Paiwan, and others — distinct from the Hoklo and Hakka majorities. Their land rights, languages (several critically endangered), and political representation are a live domestic issue almost entirely absent from international coverage of Taiwan.
The KMT's China position is complicated
Western coverage tends to frame Taiwan's politics as pro-independence DPP vs. pro-Beijing KMT. The KMT's actual position — engagement and negotiation from a distinct Taiwanese identity — is more nuanced, and the party's internal divisions between genuine "one China" advocates and Taiwanese nationalists are rarely examined.
Economic interdependence across the strait
Despite cross-strait tensions, significant numbers of Taiwanese professionals work in mainland China — in tech, finance, and manufacturing. This complicated economic interdependence, which creates real human ties across the strait, is poorly captured by coverage that treats Taiwan-China relations as purely adversarial.

🔭 South Korea's Blind Spots

The chaebol's grip on everything
Samsung, Hyundai, LG, and SK collectively account for a majority of South Korea's exports and dominate the domestic economy in ways that have no equivalent in other wealthy democracies. The chaebol's political influence — through lobbying, media ownership, and the revolving door with government — shapes almost every major policy decision in ways that Korean and international business coverage systematically understates.
Housing and the jeonse crisis
South Korea's unique jeonse rental system — large lump-sum deposits instead of monthly rent — created a housing bubble and a financial crisis when deposits couldn't be repaid. Hundreds of thousands of Koreans lost their deposits in 2022-24. This specifically Korean financial structure and its human consequences are almost never explained to international audiences.
The birth rate is not just a statistic
South Korea has the world's lowest fertility rate — around 0.7 — but coverage treats this as a demographic curiosity rather than examining why: crushing educational costs, a housing market that prices out young families, a corporate culture that expects 60-hour weeks, and deep gender inequality. Young Korean women are opting out of marriage and motherhood as a political act.
North Korea beyond the nuclear programme
International coverage of North Korea focuses almost entirely on weapons and Kim Jong-un's political theatre. South Korean media covers the daily reality of defectors, the humanitarian situation of North Korean civilians, and the slow drift of North Korean society in ways that rarely reach international outlets. The 25 million people of North Korea are almost entirely invisible as human subjects.

The Hindu covers the India-Pakistan tension as a structural failure of regional diplomacy rather than a bilateral flare-up. 🔭 India's Blind Spots — What the world misses

The farmer
India's 600 million-plus people dependent on agriculture live in a world almost entirely invisible to the English-language press — including India's own. Crop failures, debt cycles, and the slow collapse of rural livelihoods are the defining stories of the country, but they rarely surface unless there's a protest dramatic enough to block a highway.
Caste in the city
International coverage of caste focuses on rural atrocities. The urban reality — caste discrimination in hiring, housing, dating, and social networks across India's booming cities — is almost never examined, despite being the lived experience of hundreds of millions of people.
The 2024 election surprise
Modi won a third term but lost his majority — a political earthquake that Indian media covered exhaustively and international outlets largely missed. The BJP's dependence on coalition partners is now a structural constraint on policy that shapes every decision, yet most foreign coverage still treats Modi as governing with unchecked authority.

🇵🇭 Philippines — Press This Week
◀ Centre-Left / Liberal
Rappler
Digital-first investigative outlet. Founded by Nobel Peace Prize laureate Maria Ressa. Under sustained government legal pressure since 2017 but remains publishing. The most internationally recognised Philippine media brand.
Marcos-Duterte split: how the Philippines' most powerful political families went to war ▼
Ferdinand Marcos Jr. and Sara Duterte won the 2022 election as the most dominant tandem in Philippine history. By 2024 they had split — Marcos fired Duterte from cabinet, she resigned as VP in all but title, and her father Rodrigo Duterte returned from political obscurity to align against Marcos. Rappler covers this rupture as more than a personality clash: it reflects competing patronage networks, competing views on China policy, and a fundamental question about whether Philippine democracy is becoming more or less accountable. → rappler.com
South China Sea: the Philippines pushes back — and China escalates ▼
Marcos has shifted sharply from Duterte's accommodation of China toward assertive defence of Philippine maritime rights — allowing US forces expanded basing access, resuming resupply missions to the BRP Sierra Madre at Second Thomas Shoal, and publicly calling out Chinese water cannon attacks. Rappler covers this with approval but tracks the risks: China's coast guard has become increasingly aggressive, and the Philippines lacks the military hardware to enforce its claims without US backing — backing whose reliability under Trump is uncertain. → rappler.com
Maria Ressa acquitted — but the legal weaponisation of courts continues ▼
Rappler's CEO Maria Ressa spent years fighting cyber-libel charges widely condemned as politically motivated before a partial acquittal. Rappler covers press freedom as an existential issue: the Philippines has dropped sharply on press freedom indexes, journalists are among the most killed in Asia, and the legal infrastructure used against Ressa remains available for future use. The outlet is frank about its own situation — it reports its funding struggles, legal battles and editorial decisions with unusual transparency. → rappler.com

● Centre
Philippine Daily Inquirer
Philippines' largest-circulation broadsheet. Generally centrist with liberal tendencies. Critical of both Duterte and Marcos administrations on human rights. The paper the Filipino middle class reads.
Marcos's economic agenda: growth returns but inequality deepens ▼
The Philippines has returned to 6%+ GDP growth under Marcos, driven by infrastructure spending, BPO services and remittances. Philippine Daily Inquirer covers the disconnect: headline growth is real, but food inflation remains punishing for the poor, agricultural productivity is stagnant, and the jobs being created are concentrated in Metro Manila. The paper profiles the 20% of Filipinos who remain in poverty and asks whether the "Golden Age of Infrastructure" is being built for the middle class or for everyone. → inquirer.net
ICC and the drug war: Duterte faces the Hague as victims seek justice ▼
The International Criminal Court has resumed its investigation into Rodrigo Duterte's drug war — in which an estimated 6,000–30,000 people were killed in extrajudicial killings. The Inquirer covers both the legal process and the political dimension: Marcos initially withdrew from ICC proceedings, then permitted limited cooperation as his relationship with Duterte collapsed. Families of victims are a regular presence in the paper's coverage — their testimony providing a human scale to what is often framed as a geopolitical or legal abstraction. → inquirer.net
OFW economy: 10 million Filipinos abroad send home $36bn — at what human cost? ▼
The Philippines' Overseas Filipino Worker (OFW) economy is the bedrock of the country's financial stability: remittances equal roughly 9% of GDP. The Inquirer covers this with both appreciation and discomfort — profiling the families separated for years, the children raised by grandparents while parents work in the Gulf, and the systemic failure that makes emigration for domestic work the best economic option available to millions. The paper questions whether a country with this resource base should still depend so heavily on exported labour. → inquirer.net

🔭 Philippines Blind Spots — What the world misses

Climate frontline, not climate story
The Philippines is one of the world's most typhoon-exposed nations — experiencing 20+ tropical cyclones a year. International coverage treats each storm as a disaster event. Filipinos experience it as a permanent condition of life that shapes everything from infrastructure investment to internal migration to why so many leave. The political economy of climate adaptation in a poor archipelago is one of the most important and under-covered stories in Asia.
The OFW diaspora as political actor
Ten million Filipinos abroad don't just send remittances — they vote. The diaspora vote has become a meaningful factor in Philippine elections, with OFW communities in the Gulf, Hong Kong, and Italy organised, opinionated, and increasingly powerful online. Their political views don't map neatly onto domestic Philippines — they're often more liberal on social issues, more hawkish on China, and more impatient with corruption than the domestic median.

◀ Centre-Left
The Spinoff
New Zealand's leading digital-native publication. Long-form, analytical, broadly progressive. Read by urban professionals, media workers and policymakers. Strong on culture and society.
The Treaty backlash: how the coalition's Māori policies became its biggest political liability ▼
ACT's Treaty Principles Bill — which sought to redefine the Treaty of Waitangi as applying equally to all citizens, not as a partnership with Māori — triggered the largest protest march in New Zealand history in November 2024. The hikoi drew over 50,000 people to Wellington. The Spinoff's coverage frames this as a defining moment: the coalition misjudged how deeply the Treaty is embedded in New Zealand's national identity, even among non-Māori. The bill was voted down but the political damage lingers. → thespinoff.co.nz
Housing crisis unchanged: Luxon government's build targets fall short ▼
New Zealand's housing crisis — some of the world's worst affordability ratios relative to incomes — has not improved under the Luxon government despite promises to cut red tape and accelerate consenting. The Spinoff's data journalism tracks actual building completions against targets and finds a significant shortfall. The paper argues the coalition's approach (supply-side deregulation) is necessary but insufficient without demand-side intervention, particularly for first-home buyers priced out of Auckland and Wellington. → thespinoff.co.nz
Public service cuts bite: thousands of jobs gone, services degraded ▼
The Luxon government's fiscal consolidation programme eliminated over 6,000 public sector jobs in 2024 — the largest single-year reduction in decades. The Spinoff tracks the human cost: delayed consenting, slower benefit processing, reduced frontline health capacity. Government ministers frame this as right-sizing an over-bloated bureaucracy. The paper quotes former public servants who argue institutional knowledge lost in rapid redundancy rounds will take years to rebuild. → thespinoff.co.nz

🔭 New Zealand's Blind Spots — What the world misses

Pacific leadership, not Pacific tourism
New Zealand's relationship with the Pacific Islands is covered internationally as a geography and climate story. Kiwis understand it as a geopolitical responsibility, a diaspora reality (more Pacific Islanders live in Auckland than in Suva or Nukuʻalofa), and a defining part of national identity that has no equivalent in European or North American political cultures.
The Treaty is not symbolic
International media treats the Treaty of Waitangi as a historical document. In New Zealand it is a live constitutional instrument invoked in courts, woven into legislation, and debated at every election. The 2024 hikoi was not a protest — it was a constitutional assertion. That distinction is almost never understood outside New Zealand.
The housing crisis as a civilisational failure
New Zealand has some of the world's worst housing affordability despite being a small, wealthy country with vast land. This is not a market failure — it is a deliberate political choice by successive governments to protect existing homeowners. The social consequences — families in motels, young professionals emigrating — are profound and undercovered internationally.
