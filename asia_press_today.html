🇯🇵 Japan — 今日の日本の報道

◀ Centre-Left
Asahi Shimbun
Circ. ~4m · National quality broadsheet, liberal-progressive
Founded 1879 · Anti-nuclear, pacifist, critical of LDP
Liberal · Pacifist · Anti-nuclear · Investigative
Lead
Japan's alliance with America under Trump: loyalty with no guarantees
Tokyo is paying more, getting less certainty in return.
Asahi examines the strain on the US-Japan security alliance as Washington demands higher host-nation support payments while Trump questions the value of defending allies who don't carry their share. Japan has increased defence spending toward 2% of GDP — a historic shift for a constitutionally pacifist nation — but Asahi finds deep unease among the public and within the ruling coalition about where this trajectory leads. The paper warns that Japan is being asked to militarise without a genuine public debate.
asahi.com
Second
Constitutional revision: Ishiba's push to amend Article 9 meets quiet resistance
The pacifist clause has survived 80 years. Its future is suddenly uncertain.
Asahi's political desk covers the Ishiba government's renewed push to formally revise Article 9 — Japan's constitutional prohibition on maintaining war potential — to explicitly legitimise the Self-Defence Forces. Asahi, which has long championed the pacifist constitution, frames this as a fundamental change to Japan's postwar identity being rushed through without adequate public consultation, and notes poll data showing majority public unease.
asahi.com
Third
Inflation hits Japanese households — the price of ending deflation
Economists wanted prices to rise. Consumers are not celebrating.
After three decades of deflation, Japan has achieved sustained price rises — but Asahi reports that wage growth is not keeping pace for lower-income workers, particularly in rural areas and the service sector. The paper profiles households managing real income declines and asks whether the Bank of Japan's rate normalisation is moving too fast for an economy still adjusting to the concept of inflation.
asahi.com

● Centre-Right
Yomiuri Shimbun
Circ. ~8m · World's largest circulation newspaper
Founded 1874 · Pro-LDP, conservative, supports constitutional revision
Conservative · Pro-business · Pro-alliance · Pro-revision
Lead
Ishiba and Trump meet: Japan secures tariff exemptions, alliance reaffirmed
Diplomacy delivered. Japan's pragmatic approach pays off.
Yomiuri leads with what it characterises as a diplomatic success: partial exemptions from Trump's broad tariff regime secured in bilateral talks, and a joint statement reaffirming the US commitment to Japan's defence — including the nuclear umbrella. The paper presents the outcome as vindication of the Ishiba government's approach of patient engagement with Washington rather than public criticism, and contrasts it favourably with the approach of some European allies.
yomiuri.co.jp
Second
Defence spending milestone: Japan's 2% GDP commitment is right and necessary
After 80 years of pacifism, a new strategic reality demands a new posture.
Yomiuri's editorial board makes the case for Japan's historic increase in defence spending — arguing that the strategic environment (China's military buildup, North Korea's missile programme, Russia's aggression in Europe) has fundamentally changed and that Japan must shoulder a greater share of its own defence. The paper supports constitutional revision as a long-overdue recognition of this reality and frames Asahi's opposition as nostalgic and strategically naive.
yomiuri.co.jp
Third
Wage growth accelerating: Japan's economic transformation is real
Major corporates delivering the biggest pay rises in a generation.
Yomiuri's business desk reports on the spring wage negotiations (shuntō) results, with major manufacturers and service companies agreeing to wage increases averaging over 5% — the largest in 33 years. The paper argues this represents genuine structural change in Japan's economy and credits the Kishida/Ishiba growth strategy, downplaying concerns about inflation eroding real gains for lower-income workers.
yomiuri.co.jp

▶ Right / Nationalist
Sankei Shimbun
Circ. ~1.5m · Conservative-nationalist, Fuji Media group
Founded 1933 · Strongly pro-rearmament, anti-China, anti-Korea
Nationalist · Pro-military · Anti-China · Revisionist history
Lead
China's military provocations demand Japan abandon its pacifist illusions now
The threat is real and present. The constitution is a liability.
Sankei's lead editorial argues that recent Chinese naval incursions into Japanese territorial waters near the Senkaku Islands, combined with Beijing's military posture toward Taiwan, make Article 9 not merely outdated but actively dangerous. The paper calls for immediate constitutional revision, sharply increased defence spending, and a formal review of Japan's no-first-use nuclear policy — positions that would have been fringe in mainstream Japanese media a decade ago but are increasingly discussed.
sankei.com
Second
History education: Japan's schools must stop teaching self-flagellation
A nation cannot build its future by endlessly relitigating its past.
Sankei's culture desk attacks what it characterises as an entrenched "masochistic history view" in Japanese school curricula — the emphasis on wartime atrocities, comfort women, and colonial-era conduct. The paper argues that Japan has apologised sufficiently and that continued emphasis on guilt undermines national pride and strategic confidence at a moment when Japan needs to assert itself. The piece is predictably controversial and has drawn protests from South Korean and Chinese diplomatic missions.
sankei.com
Third
Immigration: Japan must resist the pressure to open its borders
Homogeneity is not racism — it is the foundation of Japan's social cohesion.
As Japan's acute labour shortage intensifies debate over immigration policy, Sankei argues firmly against significant relaxation of immigration rules, claiming Japan's low crime, high trust, and social cohesion are directly linked to its relatively homogeneous population. The paper warns against importing what it describes as the social fragmentation seen in European countries, and advocates for automation and higher birth rates as the correct response to demographic decline.

🇰🇷 South Korea — 오늘의 한국 언론

◀ Left / Progressive
Hankyoreh
Circ. ~500k digital · Founded by journalists fired under military dictatorship
Founded 1988 · Democracy-era paper, pro-civil rights, pro-engagement with North
Progressive · Pro-democracy · Pro-dialogue · Labour rights
Lead
After the martial law crisis: rebuilding constitutional democracy from the wreckage
December 2024 shook the republic. The work of repair is only beginning.
Hankyoreh reflects on the aftermath of President Yoon's short-lived martial law declaration and subsequent impeachment — characterising it as the most serious threat to South Korean democracy since the 1980s military era. The paper's editorial argues that the speed of the democratic response (the National Assembly overturning martial law within hours) proved institutional resilience, but warns that the conditions that produced the crisis — political polarisation, a president governing as if in permanent war with the opposition — have not been addressed.
hani.co.kr
Second
Youth unemployment and the housing crisis: the generation Yoon forgot
Korea's young people are locked out of housing, locked out of hope.
Hankyoreh's social affairs team reports on South Korea's acute generational crisis: youth unemployment running at 7%, apartment prices in Seoul requiring 20+ years of average salary to purchase, and birth rates at historic lows (0.72 per woman — the world's lowest). The paper draws a direct line from these economic conditions to the political instability of the Yoon era and argues the new government must treat the housing crisis as a national emergency.
hani.co.kr
Third
North Korea dialogue: the window Trump may accidentally open
Washington's unpredictability might, paradoxically, create space for talks.
Hankyoreh's foreign affairs team argues that Trump's transactional approach to alliances — and his history of direct engagement with Kim Jong-un — may create an unexpected opening for inter-Korean dialogue that a conventional US administration would not permit. The paper, which has consistently advocated engagement over pressure, urges Seoul to prepare a bold diplomatic proposal rather than simply reacting to Washington's signals.
hani.co.kr

JoongAng Ilbo
Circ. ~1.5m · Quality centrist broadsheet, Seoul-based
Founded 1965 · Part of JoongAng Media Network, business & establishment
Centrist · Pro-business · Pragmatic · Alliance-oriented
Lead
New government's first 100 days: stability restored, hard choices deferred
The crisis is over. The structural problems remain.
JoongAng Ilbo's political team assesses the early months of South Korea's post-impeachment government, finding a return to institutional normalcy but limited progress on the underlying economic and social challenges. The paper gives cautious credit for restoring calm and repairing relations with Washington after the martial law chaos, but warns that the housing crisis, youth unemployment, and demographic emergency require bolder action than the new administration has so far shown appetite for.
joongang.co.kr
Second
K-economy: the export engine is running but domestic demand is stalling
Samsung ships chips. Koreans can't afford their rent.
JoongAng's business desk presents a bifurcated picture of the Korean economy: export sectors (semiconductors, shipbuilding, electric vehicles) performing strongly, driven by global demand and Korean technological leadership, while domestic consumption remains depressed by high household debt, falling real wages, and a housing market that absorbs disposable income. The paper argues for targeted domestic stimulus without compromising the fiscal discipline that underpins Korea's sovereign credit rating.
joongang.co.kr
Third
Korea-Japan relations: the warmth is real but the history hasn't gone away
Strategic alignment is easier than emotional reconciliation.
JoongAng examines the state of Korea-Japan relations following recent summit diplomacy — finding genuine strategic convergence on China, North Korea, and the US alliance, but persistent public unease about unresolved historical issues including comfort women, forced labour, and Fukushima water releases. The paper argues pragmatically that the relationship is more important than the grievances, while acknowledging that Korean public opinion remains volatile on Japan-related issues.

Chosun Ilbo
Circ. ~2m · Korea's largest circulation daily, strongly conservative
Founded 1920 · Staunchly anti-North Korea, pro-US alliance, pro-market
Conservative · Anti-North Korea · Pro-US · National security focus
Lead
North Korea's weapons exports to Russia: the alliance Seoul cannot ignore
Pyongyang's battlefield role in Ukraine changes the calculus on the peninsula.
Chosun Ilbo's security desk leads on confirmed intelligence about North Korean artillery shells, missiles, and troops supporting Russia's war in Ukraine — arguing this represents a fundamental shift in the North's strategic posture. The paper argues Seoul must respond by deepening military cooperation with NATO, supplying Ukraine with weapons (reversing current policy), and pushing Washington for stronger extended deterrence commitments. The Russia-North Korea axis is framed as an existential threat requiring urgent action.
chosun.com
Second
The left's responsibility for the Yoon crisis: opposition overreach enabled extremism
The impeachment was right. But the left's role in the chaos deserves scrutiny too.
In a nuanced piece that distances itself from Yoon's martial law declaration — which Chosun opposed — the paper nevertheless argues that the Democratic Party's relentless use of its parliamentary majority to block the budget, impeach officials, and pursue what it calls legislative obstruction created an atmosphere of political paralysis that a weak president responded to catastrophically. The paper argues both sides of Korean politics need to examine their role in normalising constitutional brinkmanship.
chosun.com
Third
Korea's birth rate emergency: the left's welfare state won't fix a culture problem
Money alone will not make Koreans want children again.
Chosun's comment section publishes a series of pieces arguing that South Korea's catastrophic birth rate (0.72 — the world's lowest) cannot be solved by the financial incentives and childcare expansions the progressive opposition advocates. The paper argues the crisis is cultural — extreme competitiveness, education arms races, career expectations for women that make motherhood feel incompatible with professional life — and that policy solutions that don't address these root causes are expensive and ineffective.
