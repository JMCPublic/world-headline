🇳🇬 Nigeria — Press This Week
◀ Left / Centre-Left
The Punch
Nigeria's highest-circulation newspaper. Outspoken, irreverent, often critical of the Tinubu government. Strong on accountability and anti-corruption.
Fuel subsidy removal: one year on, millions still can't afford transport or cooking gas ▼
President Tinubu's shock removal of the petrol subsidy in May 2023 triggered a cost-of-living crisis that The Punch has tracked relentlessly. Pump prices quadrupled; food prices followed. The paper argues the pain has been borne almost entirely by ordinary Nigerians while elite beneficiaries of the old subsidy regime were never held accountable. It calls for direct cash transfers to offset the impact — a promise made but largely undelivered. → punchng.com
Naira devaluation has wiped out middle-class savings — CBN under fire ▼
The Central Bank of Nigeria's unification of exchange rates in 2023 sent the naira plunging, eroding savings, pushing up import prices and triggering a wave of business closures. The Punch is sharply critical of the CBN's management, questioning whether devaluation served ordinary Nigerians or currency speculators. Inflation has since eased from a peak of 34% toward 14%, but The Punch argues recovery is uneven and fragile. → punchng.com
Insecurity crisis: kidnapping, banditry and Boko Haram still claim lives across the North ▼
Mass kidnappings for ransom, armed banditry in the Northwest, and residual Boko Haram/ISWAP activity in the Northeast continue to displace millions. The Punch covers security as a governance failure — arguing the military is overstretched, underfunded and politically constrained from tackling the roots of violence: poverty, unemployment and weak local government. It is critical of the Tinubu administration for insufficient urgency. → punchng.com
● Centre
Vanguard
Widely read Lagos-based daily. Balanced news coverage with a business and development focus. Less partisan than The Punch, covers government and opposition with similar scepticism.
GDP growth at 3.98% — Nigeria's economy improving on paper, but Nigerians don't feel it ▼
Nigeria's headline GDP growth has recovered to near 4% but Vanguard consistently notes the disconnect between macro statistics and lived experience. Oil production remains below OPEC quotas due to theft and infrastructure decay; the non-oil economy is growing but from a low base. The paper calls for a credible jobs strategy targeting the 33% unemployment rate — the real measure of whether reforms are working. → vanguardngr.com
Tax reform bills signed: four major laws reshape Nigeria's revenue architecture ▼
In June 2025, President Tinubu signed four landmark tax reform bills into law, consolidating Nigeria's fragmented revenue laws, adjusting VAT thresholds and overhauling company income tax. Vanguard covers the reforms with cautious optimism — noting they were contested by Northern governors and the Labour Party, who feared reduced allocations to states. The paper urges transparent implementation and strong FIRS enforcement. → vanguardngr.com
Food security emergency: Tinubu's April 2025 declaration one year on — what changed? ▼
President Tinubu declared a food security emergency in April 2025 after a UN report found 31 million Nigerians facing acute food insecurity. Vanguard's follow-up coverage asks whether the declaration translated into meaningful action: emergency food imports were approved, grain reserves were released, but farmgate prices remain high and insecurity continues to displace farmers in the country's breadbasket regions. → vanguardngr.com
▶ Right / Pro-Business
ThisDay
Lagos-based business and politics daily. Generally supportive of Tinubu's economic reforms. Voice of Nigeria's corporate and financial establishment. Close ties to APC policy circles.
Reforms are working: inflation falls to 14.45%, foreign investment returning to Nigeria ▼
ThisDay frames the Tinubu economic programme as a difficult but necessary structural transformation. It highlights inflation falling from a 34% peak to around 14% by November 2025, improved CBN credibility after exchange-rate unification, and renewed interest from foreign investors in Nigerian bonds and equities. The paper argues the pain of 2023–24 was the price of ending decades of economic distortions. → thisdaylive.com
Lagos free trade zone and Lekki Deep Sea Port driving new economic corridors ▼
ThisDay covers Nigeria's infrastructure and investment story enthusiastically. The Lekki Deep Sea Port, now fully operational, has begun to reduce the chronic congestion at Apapa that added billions to Nigeria's logistics costs. Combined with the Lagos free trade zone, ThisDay argues Nigeria is building the physical infrastructure of a competitive emerging market — if the policy environment holds. → thisdaylive.com
2027 election landscape: APC must consolidate reform gains to retain the presidency ▼
With the 2027 general elections approaching, ThisDay is tracking the political landscape carefully. It frames Tinubu's path to re-election as dependent on whether economic improvements become visible to voters before the campaign — lower inflation, more stable naira, and security gains in the North. The paper is broadly supportive of the APC position but urges the party to widen its coalition beyond Lagos and the Southwest. → thisdaylive.com

🔦 Nigeria's Blind Spots

🌾 Herders-farmers conflict: The chronic violence between Fulani herdsmen and farming communities in the Middle Belt kills thousands annually but receives minimal sustained coverage relative to its scale. Ethnic and religious sensitivities make editors cautious, and the story rarely reaches international attention despite its enormous human cost.
🛢️ Oil theft and elite capture: Nigeria loses an estimated 200,000 barrels per day to pipeline theft. The networks involved reach into the military and political class. Investigative reporting on this is rare; journalists who pursue it face serious risk — and the scale of the loss represents a structural drain on the Nigerian state.
⚖️ LGBTQ+ rights and the criminalisation gap: Nigeria's Same-Sex Marriage Prohibition Act (2014) criminalises same-sex relationships with up to 14 years in prison. Press coverage is almost uniformly hostile; human rights dimensions are largely absent from mainstream reporting, and international coverage treats this as a side issue rather than a systematic rights emergency.

🔦 Ghana's Blind Spots — What the world misses

🌊 Coastal erosion crisis: Ghana is losing up to 2 metres of coastline per year in some areas — entire fishing communities are relocating. Rarely makes international news despite being an accelerating climate emergency.
🌍 Afrocontinental migration pressure: Ghana is both a source and destination country for West African migration. The internal dynamics — economic migrants from Mali and Burkina Faso fleeing instability — are reshaping Ghana's social fabric in ways the global press entirely overlooks.
📱 Fintech leadership: Ghana is one of Africa's most advanced mobile money markets. The interoperability of its payment systems — allowing transfers across networks — is a model being studied globally, but the story rarely escapes the specialist press.

🔦 Senegal's Blind Spots — What the world misses

🛶 The pirogue crisis: Thousands of young Senegalese risk death annually crossing the Atlantic to Spain's Canary Islands. 2023–24 saw record crossings and drownings. This is one of West Africa's most urgent humanitarian stories and receives only episodic international coverage.
🗳️ Senegal's democratic rupture: Senegal was held up for decades as West Africa's model democracy. The 2023–24 crisis — Sonko's prosecution, mass protests, internet shutdowns, and Sall's attempted electoral manipulation — shattered that narrative. The story behind the story is rarely told internationally.
🛢️ First oil — and the resource curse question: Senegal began oil production in 2024. Whether it becomes a Norway or a Nigeria will be determined by institutional choices made now. The international press has covered the discovery; it has barely begun covering the governance question.
🕌 The Mouride Brotherhood's political economy: Senegal's powerful Sufi Muslim brotherhoods — especially the Mourides, centred on the holy city of Touba — operate a parallel economy, command mass loyalty, and influence every election. This is invisible in Western coverage of Senegalese politics.

🔦 South Africa's Blind Spots — What the world misses

🌊 Water crisis: Johannesburg — Africa's wealthiest city — regularly runs out of water due to crumbling infrastructure and an ageing Rand Water system. This is not a drought story; it's an infrastructure collapse story that the international press consistently undercoveres.
🌍 SA as Africa's anchor democracy: South Africa's political trajectory matters far beyond its borders. If the GNU fails and ANC-populist alliances entrench, the ripple effects across Southern Africa will be significant — but the world's media covers SA as a local story, not a continental bellwether.
💡 Rooftop solar revolution: Load shedding triggered an extraordinary private solar uptake — SA added more rooftop solar per capita than almost any country in the world in 2023–24. This clean energy transformation is happening organically, from the bottom up, and is largely invisible to international energy commentators.

Kenya's largest-circulation daily. Part of Nation Media Group. Broadly independent, often critical of the Ruto government. Strong on accountability, civil liberties and economic fairness.
One year after the Gen Z uprising: Finance Bill is dead but the grievances live on ▼
The June 2024 protests — in which young Kenyans stormed parliament and forced the withdrawal of the Finance Bill — were a watershed moment. Daily Nation's anniversary coverage asks whether anything changed. Ruto scrapped the bill, reshuffled his cabinet and promised austerity. Debt trap: Kenya spends more on debt repayment than on health and education combined ▼
Kenya's external debt has ballooned to over $80bn — roughly 70% of GDP. Daily Nation's economics desk argues the IMF programme, while stabilising the shilling, has locked Kenya into a structural squeeze: austerity preserves debt repayment but deepens the cost-of-living crisis. Housing levy fury: workers ask why their pay is funding Ruto's political flagship ▼
Ruto's Affordable Housing Programme levies 1.5% of every worker's gross salary — matched by employers. The Supreme Court initially ruled it unconstitutional but a subsequent amendment kept it alive. The paper notes that most beneficiaries in the initial allocations were civil servants, not the urban poor the programme claimed to target. → nation.africa

🔦 Kenya's Blind Spots — What the world misses

📱 M-Pesa as infrastructure: Mobile money didn't just transform banking in Kenya — it became the operating system for the economy. Merchants, farmers, landlords and governments transact through it. The international tech press covers M-Pesa as a fintech story; Kenyans experience it as basic infrastructure. 🌊 The Gen Z generation as a political force: The June 2024 protests were not a one-off. They marked the emergence of a genuinely post-tribal, digitally-organised Kenyan youth vote that cut across ethnic lines for the first time in the country's history. International coverage treated this as a riot story. Kenyan media understands it as a generational political realignment whose implications will play out over decades.
🌍 Raila Odinga's continental role: Odinga's installation as AU Commission Chairperson in 2025 gives Kenya direct influence over Africa's primary multilateral institution.
