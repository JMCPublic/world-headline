Khan blocks Palantir deal amid police surveillance technology concerns
London's mayor stands firm against expanding AI-driven surveillance powers, rejecting a controversial tech partnership that could reshape policing.
Sadiq Khan has blocked a £50 million contract between the Metropolitan Police and Palantir, the US surveillance technology firm with a contested track record. Scotland Yard has publicly criticised the decision, claiming it will damage policing capabilities, but Khan's intervention reflects growing concerns about automated intelligence systems and corporate control over law enforcement data. The conflict highlights the ongoing tension between technological expansion and democratic accountability in security services.theguardian.com

HS2 spirals to £102bn as government clings to sunk-cost delusion
The government remains trapped by the sunk-cost fallacy, pouring billions into a rail project that should be scrapped in favour of urban transit investment.
Transport Secretary Heidi Alexander confirmed that HS2 costs have ballooned to £102.7 billion with trains now unlikely to run before 2039, yet the government continues defending the project. Simon Jenkins argues this represents a catastrophic failure of rational decision-making, where political commitment to past choices prevents reassessment of whether those choices still make sense. The Observer's analysis suggests redirecting these funds toward regional transport solutions would deliver far greater public benefit than completing an outdated megaproject.theguardian.com

🔭 The World Doesn't See This — Britain's Blind Spots

The Labour civil war
97 MPs calling for the PM to go barely registered outside Britain. Internationally, Starmer is seen as a stable if uninspiring figure. Inside the UK it feels like a full-blown crisis.
Welsh politics
Labour losing control of Wales for the first time in 100 years is huge in British political history. Almost no international outlet covered it.
NHS as moral symbol
The NHS drug price row matters to other countries as a trade fact. In Britain it's a near-religious debate about national identity and what the state owes its citizens.
Reform's momentum
Farage is a figure of mild curiosity abroad. In Britain his party is now polling neck-and-neck with Labour — a genuinely historic shift that foreign press has largely missed.
