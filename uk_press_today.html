The Prime Minister's self-erasing messages obscure scrutiny of his Mandelson appointment amid already opaque government record-keeping.
Downing Street has confirmed that Keir Starmer's WhatsApp messages automatically delete, complicating efforts to understand the full picture of his decision-making around Peter Mandelson's appointment. The revelation compounds concerns about transparency in government at a time when recently released files are already raising questions about the Prime Minister's role in senior appointments. Such digital practices risk creating accountability gaps precisely when public scrutiny of executive decisions should be most thorough.theguardian.com

Forever chemicals threaten Scotland's remote island water supplies
Fair Isle's alarming PFAS contamination exposes how toxic pollution reaches even Britain's most isolated communities.
Scientists have identified concerning levels of "forever chemicals" (PFAS) in Fair Isle's drinking water, making the remote Scottish island one of the most contaminated sites in the country. Researchers believe seafoam drifting across the isolated community may be the source, raising urgent questions about how synthetic pollutants travel globally and accumulate in unexpected places. The discovery should serve as a warning to other coastal areas vulnerable to PFAS contamination from industrial and consumer products.theguardian.com

🔭 The World Doesn't See This — Britain's Blind Spots

The Labour civil war
97 MPs calling for the PM to go barely registered outside Britain. Internationally, Starmer is seen as a stable if uninspiring figure. Inside the UK it feels like a full-blown crisis.
Welsh politics
Labour losing control of Wales for the first time in 100 years is huge in British political history. Almost no international outlet covered it.
NHS as moral symbol
The NHS drug price row matters to other countries as a trade fact. In Britain it's a near-religious debate about national identity and what the state owes its citizens.
Reform's momentum
Farage is a figure of mild curiosity abroad. In Britain his party is now polling neck-and-neck with Labour — a genuinely historic shift that foreign press has largely missed.
