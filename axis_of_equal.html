🌍 Best Place to Be

Pick your political identity — see which countries have the strongest mainstream representation for your views, ranked by how closely a real party matches your position on the spectrum.
⚠ Why some countries aren't here
The Axis of Equal can only chart countries with genuine multiparty competition and a press that operates independently of the state. Countries where the government controls political outcomes or suppresses opposition can't be honestly placed on a spectrum — their "parties" don't represent real public political positions.
🇨🇳 China — one-party state 🇷🇺 Russia — managed democracy 🇸🇦 Saudi Arabia — no parties 🇰🇵 North Korea — totalitarian 🇮🇷 Iran — theocratic control 🇧🇾 Belarus — authoritarian 🇹🇲 Turkmenistan — one-party 🇪🇷 Eritrea — no elections

🔍 Country Comparison

How do countries compare on the political spectrum? All rankings are computed live from the party positions you see in the chart above — weighted to give governing parties slightly more influence over the national mean.
How are party positions on the spectrum decided?
Every party's position is an editorial assessment — not an algorithm. Positions are derived from a combination of:
📄 Party manifestosWhat the party formally commits to in its official programme
🗳️ Voting recordsHow the party actually votes in parliament on key economic and social legislation
📰 Press coverageHow the party is positioned relative to others by independent journalists in its own country
🌐 International comparisonsCross-country calibration — ensuring a "centre-right" party in one country sits consistently relative to parties with similar positions elsewhere

The scale runs 0 → 10: 0 = furthest left (communist/statist), 5 = centre, 10 = furthest right (fascist/ultranationalist). Party positions are mapped to percentages internally (so position 4.4 = 44% from the left edge).

What this is not: These positions are not based on self-identification (parties routinely misdescribe themselves), polling of party supporters, or purely economic criteria. The scale captures the full left-right dimension including economic policy, social conservatism, and attitude to liberal democratic norms.

Disagree with a placement? The positions are deliberately open to challenge — that's part of the point. The goal is honest comparison, not official endorsement of any position.
How is Best Place to Be scored?
Each party on the chart is given a spectrum position from 0 to 100 — 0 is the furthest left, 100 the furthest right, 50 is the centre. These positions are editorial assessments based on manifestos, stated policies, and voting records.

When you pick a political identity, it maps to an ideal position on that same scale (e.g. Social Democrat = 38, Liberal/Centrist = 50, Traditional Conservative = 73). The tool then looks at every country and finds the party whose position is closest to your ideal.

Score formula: max(0, round(100 − distance × 2.2))
A perfect match scores 100. Each point of distance costs 2.2 points. A party 45 points away scores 1; any further and the country drops off the list entirely.
