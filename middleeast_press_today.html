Haaretz
Israel's most internationally read daily. Liberal, anti-occupation, fiercely critical of Netanyahu. The paper the government hates most.

Haaretz argues that Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich and National Security Minister Itamar Ben-Gvir — both far-right — have effectively captured Netanyahu's government. The paper's central thesis: Netanyahu needs them to survive politically and legally, so they set the real agenda. Gaza policy, West Bank settler violence, and judicial overhaul are all, in Haaretz's reading, driven by the far right's demands rather than any strategic calculus. → haaretz.com

Haaretz has been the most consistent Israeli outlet in reporting casualty figures, displacement, and aid blockages in Gaza that the government disputes or minimises. The paper publishes accounts from field workers, UNRWA staff, and international medical teams. Its Gaza coverage is why it is simultaneously Israel's most internationally cited newspaper and the one the government most wants to silence. → haaretz.com

Haaretz has run a sustained editorial line arguing that Netanyahu's government has repeatedly prioritised coalition survival over achieving a hostage deal. The paper gives prominent coverage to hostage families who have turned against the government, and has published several investigations into moments when a deal was reportedly close but talks collapsed due to Israeli demands shaped by far-right pressure. → haaretz.com

● Centre
Yedioth Ahronoth
Israel's highest-circulation print daily. Centrist-populist, once pro-Netanyahu, now sharply critical. Reflects mainstream Israeli public anxiety.
The hostage deal: what we know, what's being offered, and why it keeps falling apart ▼
Yedioth Ahronoth's coverage of hostage negotiations is the most closely followed in Israel — its reporters have senior contacts on all sides of the mediation. The paper presents the deal framework in granular detail and has consistently reported that the main obstacle is not Hamas's demands but disagreements within the Israeli cabinet over whether to accept a permanent ceasefire as part of the arrangement. → ynetnews.com
Economy on war footing: the cost of 18 months of conflict and who is paying it ▼
Israel's economy has absorbed a significant shock from the ongoing conflict — defence spending has surged, tourism has collapsed, foreign investment has slowed, and the shekel has weakened. Yedioth Ahronoth frames this as a middle-class story: the cost of living up, social services squeezed, and reservists' families carrying a disproportionate burden while the ultra-Orthodox exemption from military service remains politically untouchable. → ynetnews.com
Protest movement: the Saturday night rallies haven't stopped — and the government still isn't listening ▼
Weekly mass protests — calling for a hostage deal, early elections, and an end to the judicial overhaul — have continued since October 2023. Yedioth Ahronoth covers these as genuine expressions of mainstream Israeli frustration, distinct from the left-wing framing the government applies. The paper notes that protest organisers now include bereaved families, reservists, and business owners who voted for Netanyahu in the past. → ynetnews.com

🔭 Israel's Blind Spots — What the world misses

The ultra-Orthodox question
Israel's fastest-growing population group — Haredi Jews — largely does not serve in the military, does not participate in the formal economy, and receives state subsidies while secular Israelis carry the tax and defence burden. This internal social fracture is as consequential as any external conflict but almost never appears in international coverage of Israel.
Israel's African and Mizrahi majority
Over half of Israeli Jews trace their origin to Middle Eastern and North African countries — not Europe. The Ashkenazi/Mizrahi cultural divide shapes Israeli politics profoundly, including why working-class Mizrahi voters back Netanyahu despite economic pressures. This dimension is almost entirely absent from Western coverage.
Tech sector exodus
Hundreds of Israeli tech workers and startups have quietly relocated to Portugal, Cyprus, and the UK since 2023 — combining war risk, judicial uncertainty, and military reserve duty into a calculation that makes leaving rational. The long-term brain drain risk to Israel's innovation economy is a story almost no international outlet is tracking.

Wounded but not defeated: the resistance rebuilds its position in the south ▼
Al-Akhbar pushes back against the narrative that Hezbollah's 2024 losses — including the death of Hassan Nasrallah — represent a permanent strategic defeat. The paper covers IMF reform negotiations with deep suspicion, arguing that Western-backed conditionality historically protects financial elites while imposing austerity on working people. → al-akhbar.com

Al-Akhbar frames the Aoun-Salam government's relationship with Washington and the Gulf states as a test of whether Lebanon can resist external diktat. The paper is particularly hostile to any US or Israeli conditions on reconstruction funding, and frames pressure to disarm Hezbollah as part of a regional agenda to eliminate the last effective armed force opposing Israeli expansion. Coverage is explicitly from the resistance bloc's perspective. → al-akhbar.com

L'Orient Today approaches the new government with cautious hope — the first competent, internationally respected leadership Lebanon has had in decades. Billions in reconstruction pledges are flowing toward southern Lebanon. L'Orient Today asks the questions donors aren't asking: how will contracts be awarded, who will oversee the money, and will reconstruction reinforce Hezbollah's patronage networks or genuinely transfer to state institutions? The paper argues this is where the battle between the old Lebanon and the new one will actually be decided — not in political speeches. → lorienttoday.com

🔭 Lebanon's Blind Spots — What the world misses

The banking heist
Over $100 billion in Lebanese depositors' savings was effectively stolen through a state-sanctioned Ponzi scheme run by the commercial banks and Banque du Liban. The bank owners transferred wealth abroad while ordinary Lebanese lost everything. No senior banker has been prosecuted. This is one of the largest financial crimes in the modern Middle East and receives a fraction of the coverage it deserves.
Confessionalism as the system
International coverage describes Lebanon's sectarian divisions as a problem to be solved. Lebanese scholars describe confessionalism as a deliberately maintained system that benefits every sectarian elite by preventing genuine accountability. Understanding this distinction — dysfunction as design, not accident — is essential to understanding why reform is so hard.
1.5 million Syrian refugees
Lebanon hosts the world's highest per-capita refugee population. The economic, social, and political pressures this creates — particularly in a collapsed economy — are enormous, and the path to resolution (Syrian return, local integration, third-country resettlement) is completely unclear. This story almost never appears in coverage focused on Lebanon's political elite.
The Beirut explosion's unfinished reckoning
The August 2020 port explosion killed over 200 people, injured 6,000, and displaced 300,000. Five years on, the judicial investigation has been repeatedly blocked by politicians using parliamentary immunity. No one has been held accountable. The families' ongoing legal fight is one of the most important stories in the Arab world — and one of the least covered.

🇸🇦 Saudi Arabia — Press This Week | الصحافة هذا الأسبوع
◆ English / Pro-reform
Arab News
Circ. ~130,000 · Saudi Arabia's flagship English-language daily. Owned by Saudi Research and Media Group (SRMG), connected to the Al Saud royal family. The international face of Saudi Arabia's press: cosmopolitan in tone, enthusiastic about Vision 2030, read by diplomats, expats, and the international business community. Represents the "Saudi Arabia is changing" narrative.
Vision 2030 at the halfway mark: what's been built, what's been delayed, and what was always a mirage ▼
Arab News publishes its mid-point assessment of Vision 2030, the programme launched in 2016 to diversify Saudi Arabia away from oil by 2030. The paper highlights genuine progress — the entertainment sector, women in the workforce, NEOM groundwork, the Saudi Pro League's global signings. It largely sidesteps the harder questions: NEOM's $500bn budget has ballooned with no clear path to completion; the promised private-sector jobs haven't materialised at scale; and oil still accounts for over 60% of government revenue. Arab News frames the story as a journey not yet complete, rather than a plan running into structural reality. → arabnews.com
Saudi Arabia's Gaza position: threading the needle between the Arab street and Washington ▼
Arab News covers Saudi Arabia's carefully calibrated response to the Gaza war: public condemnation of civilian casualties, humanitarian aid pledges, and sustained calls for a ceasefire — while keeping back-channel lines to Washington and Tel Aviv intact. MBS had been close to a normalisation deal with Israel before October 7 derailed it; Arab News reflects the official position that normalisation remains a long-term strategic goal, but requires a Palestinian state pathway first. The paper's coverage carefully avoids the word "occupation" and frames Saudi diplomacy as constructive rather than complicit. → arabnews.com
Saudisation targets and the workforce gap: can Vision 2030 work without the workers? ▼
Saudi Arabia's economy runs on 13 million migrant workers who fill jobs many Saudi nationals don't want, won't take at offered wage rates, or lack the training for. Nitaqat (Saudisation) quotas require companies to hire Saudi employees, but compliance is often cosmetic — Saudi names on payrolls, foreign workers doing the jobs. Arab News covers the government's response: new training programmes, "human capital" initiatives, and upbeat statistics on Saudi female workforce participation, which has genuinely risen. The deeper structural problem — that the social contract of state employment and subsidies has not prepared a generation for private-sector work — goes unexamined. → arabnews.com

The paper covers the Houthi threat to shipping in the Red Sea as both a security crisis and an example of Iran's diminished but still active capacity for regional disruption through proxies. 🔭 Saudi Arabia's Blind Spots — What the world misses

The Kafala system and 13 million invisible workers
Saudi Arabia runs on the labour of roughly 13 million migrant workers — from South Asia, East Africa, and Southeast Asia — under a sponsorship system that ties workers to a single employer who controls their right to change jobs, exit the country, or seek legal redress. Passport confiscation, unpaid wages, physical abuse, and deaths in custody have been extensively documented by Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International. These workers build the towers, drive the cars, clean the hospitals, and raise the children of Saudi Arabia's middle and upper class. Their conditions are almost entirely absent from Saudi media and from most international coverage focused on Vision 2030's modernisation story.
The succession problem nobody will name
Mohammed bin Salman is Crown Prince and Prime Minister — the effective ruler of Saudi Arabia — but he is not king. His father, King Salman, is 89 and in uncertain health. The question of what happens when Salman dies — whether MBS becomes king smoothly, whether rival branches of the royal family accept this, and what it means for the institutional basis of MBS's power — is the central unspoken question in Saudi politics. No Saudi publication will explore it. Few international ones do either.
Vision 2030's structural contradiction
The programme requires a diverse private sector and a skilled Saudi workforce willing to take private-sector jobs. But Saudi Arabia's social contract is built on government employment, subsidies, and a welfare state funded by oil. The generation that needs to staff the private economy has been shaped by expectations of state employment at generous wages. Saudisation quotas produce compliance, not transformation: Saudi names on payrolls, foreign workers doing the work. This gap between ambition and social reality is Vision 2030's central unexamined challenge.
Yemen and the accountability that never came
Saudi Arabia led a coalition air campaign over Yemen from 2015 that killed hundreds of thousands of people, deliberately bombed civilian infrastructure including hospitals and water systems, and contributed to one of the world's worst humanitarian crises. The Saudi-Iranian deal paused the fighting. But no inquiry, no reckoning, and no honest public examination of what the war was actually for has taken place — in Saudi Arabia, in the West whose arms and intelligence made it possible, or in most international media.
